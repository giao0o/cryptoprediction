Peer Review Presentation

Constrained ETF Portfolio Optimization via Mixed-Integer Quadratic Programming

Reviewing the work of Group 1: Baumann, Tran, Li

TUM School of Management Peer Review Session

Paper Summary

Objective

To construct an optimal ETF portfolio that balances risk and return while enforcing realistic operational constraints (diversification, cardinality, costs).

Data

  • Universe: 120 iShares ETFs (Liquid).
  • Source: Web-scraped data from Yahoo Finance (Prices) & iShares (Holdings).
  • Period: 6 years of historical data.

Methodology: MIQP

The group formulated the problem as a Mixed-Integer Quadratic Program to solve for specific constraints:

  • Cardinality: Max 10 ETFs.
  • Diversification: Max 30% per Country/Sector.
  • Concentration: Max 50% per ETF.
  • Cost: Penalty for High TER.
TUM School of Management Paper Overview

Methodological Strengths

1. Mathematical Rigor

The use of MIQP is a sophisticated choice. Standard Mean-Variance optimizers cannot handle "Counting Constraints" (e.g., exactly 10 stocks). The group correctly identified that non-convex constraints require integer programming.

2. Reproducible Pipeline

The data collection strategy is commendable:

  • Avoided "Black Box" datasets.
  • Built a custom scraper for iShares holdings data.
  • Mapped industry/region exposure manually, ensuring high data granularity.
Verdict: The technical implementation of the optimization engine and data pipeline is the strongest part of this paper.
TUM School of Management Strengths

Critique I: In-Sample Evaluation

The paper reports impressive results (Sharpe Ratio 0.95 vs Benchmark 0.66), but explicitly states:

"All reported performance metrics are in-sample... intended for structural comparison." (Section 6)

Why this is problematic:

  • The optimizer "knew" the future. It selected Gold Producers and Tech because they historically performed well.
  • It does not prove the model can predict future winners, only that it can fit past data efficiently.

Look-Ahead Bias

Without an Out-of-Sample (Rolling Window) test, the performance metrics are essentially overfitted.

A simple "Buy and Hold" of the best past performers would likely yield similar results without the complex MIQP.

TUM School of Management Critique: Evaluation

Critique II: Static Framework

Single-Period Optimization

The model solves for a single optimal portfolio over 6 years.

  • Reality Check: Portfolios drift. Weights change as prices move.
  • Rebalancing: The paper does not account for rebalancing frequency or transaction costs.

Instability Risks

MIQP solutions can be unstable ("Bang-Bang" solutions). A small change in expected returns can cause the solver to swap one ETF for another entirely.

Example from Paper:
The Low Risk portfolio holds 50% MSCI Europe.
The Medium Risk portfolio holds 0% MSCI Europe.

Would an investor really sell their entire European position just because their risk tolerance shifted slightly?

TUM School of Management Critique: Implementation

Discussion Questions

Question 1: Robustness

If you ran a Walk-Forward Validation (e.g., train on Year 1-3, test on Year 4), do you believe the MIQP would still outperform the MSCI World benchmark? Or would the turnover costs eat the alpha?

Question 2: Cardinality

You set K=10 for the max number of ETFs. How sensitive is the Sharpe Ratio to this number? If K=5, does performance degrade significantly? This would test the value of diversification.

Final Assessment

Criteria Rating Comments
Methodology Strong MIQP formulation; handles constraints well.
Data Quality Excellent reproducible scraping pipeline.
Evaluation Weak due to lack of Out-of-Sample testing.
Conclusion: A technically sound framework that needs rigorous backtesting to prove its economic value.
